Wireframing for Your Designing Process

Jul 4th, 2015

A client has come to you with a project, or you have an idea for an app or a website. That’s awesome! But before you jump into photoshop or sketch and start designing what the interface will look like, you first need to think through a few things:

1. What are your goals?
- Is the content the focus, like a blog or news reader?
- Is your goal to sell a product?
- Do you have a large amount of content you need to figure out the best way to layout?
And based on your goals…

2. What is the layout & structure of your app or site?
- Will this be a full width fluid layout, or a fixed width?
- Should you have a sidebar, or keep it a single column.
- Where’s your navigation going?

3. How do you want your users to flow through the site?
- What’s the most important action a user should take on this page?
- If they click on a certain element, what will happen?
- Do you want your user to scroll and read through your content, or do you want them to immediately click a call to action button to go to another page?

This is where wireframing comes in…

What is wireframing?

Wireframing is the visual representation of an interface made up of only simple shapes to represent various elements of the app or website. They are void of any design elements such as images, colors or fonts. You can think of a wireframe as the blueprint for your app or website.

Wireframes are used to communicate information hierarchy, structure and content as well as the functionality and behavior of an interface.

The reason Wireframing is such an important step in any design is because it allows you to spot any potential issues in the structure or flow of the interface before you spend a whole bunch of time crafting the look and feel. It’s much easier to change a wireframe than a full mockup.

Wireframing Process

1. User Flow
This step is to define the flow of your app or website based on the criteria you or your client provide. For example, a client could ask to have the following pages on their site, all with the goal of pushing users to contact them:
- Recent work
- Testimonials
- Recent blog post
- History of the company timeline
- Team members with bios
- Contact form

You could mock it up something like this:

This allows you (along with your team and/or client) to have a birds eye view of the site. They’ll see what happens when a user clicks on certain elements, what content is going to be needed, and what pages need to be created.

Be sure not to get caught up in the details of layout at this stage. It doesn’t matter if you want the recent work stacked, or in a grid, as long as its represented in the mockup.

2. Low Fidelity Wireframes
A low fidelity wireframe is basically a brain dump of ideas. Usually done with pen & paper (I like to use a sharpie because of the thick lines, to make sure I don’t add unnecessary details at this point), these quick sketches are used for rapid iteration of ideas and is used for 100% layout & structure at this stage.

Usually done with pen & paper (I like to use a sharpie because of the thick lines, to make sure I don’t add unnecessary details at this point), these quick sketches are used for rapid iteration of ideas and is used for 100% layout & structure at this stage.

4. High Fidelity Wireframes
A high fidelity wireframe is as close as you’ll get to the final mockup without having the final colors or images. At this stage, you’ll use actual copy & icons that will be in the final design (Assuming you have them, use filler text if not).

5. The Final Mockup
And finally, after the many iterations between your team or between you & your client, you can create the final design.

You can see how each step of the wireframing process helps you to make informed design decisions as you progress, ultimately leading you do a beautiful design that has been vetted for any UX issues and gives the client exactly what they were looking for.
